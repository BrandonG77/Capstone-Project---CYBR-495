## What Is Incident Response?

Incident response in cybersecurity is similar to the way a firefighter battles a fire. Similar to the countless types of fires, ways they start, and steps for battling them, there are multiple ways a cybersecurity attack can occur, various types of attacks, and differing levels of severity of each one. Each organization--large or small--should have a designated Incident Response Lead who focuses on securing the company's resources. Resources can be anything computer-related, person-related, or anything else that the organization depends on to be successful.

Despite small companies having just a handful of employees or departments, having an effective plan for responding to cybersecurity incidents is detrimental to the business's livelihood. Just one data breach or system outage can wipe a company from existence--much like a fire can to any structure.

By using effective incident response procedures, organizations can protect themselves against today's most critical cybersecurity threats even without an entire security department. Dedicating even one employee to this role will help a business overcome cybersecurity challenges.

---

## Why Is Documentation Important?

Even with someone who's responsible for a company's incident response plan, their effort is essentially useless if it isn't well documented. What if something happens to that individual or the place they're storing it is destroyed? Documenting an organization's resources, assets, employees and respective customers, processes, workflows, and endless "how to" steps give an Incident Response Plan the power it needs to defend a company.

In terms of what documentation should exist for incident response, the needs vary greatly based on the industry and size of the organization. However, at a minimum, one's technical alert notifications, impacted users and systems, how the attack occurred and why it happened, communication between employees and customers who are affected, steps taken to contain the issue and ways to prevent future occurrences, and a post-incident review to summarize the attack from beginning to end should be included. These elements keep facts straight about the business's preparedness against cybersecurity threats and can be referenced in the future.

## How To Read These Incidents

Each incident response follows the same procedures:

- **Detection Alert** — how each incident is detected and common steps to respond to each one
- **Incident Ticket** — a mock incident ticket for tracking critical information for each case
- **Investigation** — parts of a company or system an attacker can use to carry out the attack
- **Stakeholder Communication** — internal and external communication templates used to inform stakeholders of the situation
- **Containment** — methods for mitigating the risk of the attack occurring again
- **Post-Incident Review** — a summary of the incident's lifespan to identify areas needing improvement in future attack occurrences

## Key Takeaway For Small Businesses

A company doesn't require an entire squad of IT employees to successfully defend itself against cybersecurity threats. Even assigning one person as the Incident Response Lead for the business makes a lasting impact on the organization's outcome. Staying up-to-date on current security threats pertainant to the company's industry can keep it ahead of the game without falling victim to security failures.

While the incident response examples in this playbook aren't comprehensive of a real-world business environment, they provide a fundamental structure to be built off of as a small business grows. With that being said, maintain up-to-date documentation on relevant cybersecurity incidents, monitor each event as thoroughly as possible, and be proactive about protecting organizations from impacting threats.
